Amazon learning tool - redesign
How to assign training
in an intuitive way

UX designer

Development
How to improve the usability
of the current Amazon learning tool?
Today Amazon uses 9 different Learning Management Systems (LMSs) to stay compliant with policy-mandatory training. Each of these systems is a 3rd party product and they are very complex to use. That’s why Amazon decided to create its first federated LMS.
In 2020, they started to develop a Beta version of this tool, which had some problems. Therefore, in 2022 Amazon decided to start the redesign of this version. My main requirement was to make the UI consistent with the rest of Amazon's internal products using an existing design system, but I also wanted to see if the current UX solved the customers' problems.
1. Define customers' problems
The customers of the experience I worked on are learning administrators. Today they have to perform 2 different tasks: assign training to a group of amazon employees and check if these trainings are correctly assigned. From a previous research, the product team identified 2 core problems.
  1. Assign incorrectly trainings: from 2018 this cost $5M in time lost in taking unnecessary training.
  2. Poor customer experience: the System Usability Score of the current experience was 75/100, which is good but it could be improved
2. Why do admins assign incorrect training?
To understand the problem, I run 2 working sessions with the Tech Product Manager to analyse the flow.
Customers found as most challenging step throughout the workflow the step of selecting a group of learners based on up to 17 different types of criteria (e.g. Job level, company, work location...)
3. Core problems of selecting learners
I identified 2 core problems of the step of selecting learners:
  • Use of unfamiliar language and lack of guidance: this creates difficulties for customers in selecting the right criteria, thus selecting the right group of learners.
  • Unable to check the number of learners selected: admins could easily submit the assignment and assign the training to 30000 learners, even if they only wanted to assign it to 500.
4. Industry benchmarking
I analysed how other companies select a wide range of population (e.g. Google, Facebook, Jira). I found out that they use 2 main different approaches:
  1. Select criteria approach: it uses 3 field to select a condition and unfamiliar language
  2. Select audience approach: it groups the category and it uses a natural language
5. Design exploration and reviews
I created 2 core design explorations based on the 2 approaches analysed from the industry benchmarking and I reviewed them with the design team, product team and the accessibility consultant.
After the reviews I opted to follow the “select criteria” approach because admins were more familiar with this pattern, but I improved it to make it also easy-to-use for first-time users.
6. Usability testing
With the UX researcher, I conducted a Usability Testing with 9 admins. The System Usability Score of the new design was excellent. It increased of 7.5 points from the current experience, solving the problem of a poor Customer experience.
One of the feedback we received was that first-time users still need more guidance, so I implemented a hint card: a card that will appear only for first-time users. It provides more info about the page and it links the FAQ page.
7. Leadership Team presentation & launch
After the definition of the design I had the possibility to present the new prototype to the leadership team (5+ directors). They easily understood the flow: they didn’t have any questions regarding the scenario.
I collaborate with the tech team and the accessibility expert to provide guidance on the development of the product which is launched in January 2023.
